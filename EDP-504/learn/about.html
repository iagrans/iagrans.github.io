An Overview of Technology Enahnced Learning

This website is about technologies for learning and teaching. These technologies range from consumer technologies like PowerPoint all the way to Game Based Learning Environments. Educators are aware of technology's potential for changing how learning and teaching take place. Technology is becoming increasingly pervasive in today's society (banks, grocery stores, smart phones) and can contribute and perhaps revolutoinize how students learn. On this website we hope to highlight different kinds of learning technologies and examine the implications of cognitive psychology (how people think and learn) for the use and design of these technolgies [1].

Much of Educational Psychology (the study of how people learn) lies in the realm of Cognitive Psychology (the study of mental processes such as attention, memory, and problem solving). Research has shown that technology itself is not the contributing to increased learning gains, but rahter good instructional strategies that take advantage of what technologies have to offer [2]. This takes into account questions like "Should students work with technology alone or collaboratively?", "Can technology promote educational equity?" and "How can this facilitate classroom discussion?" What follows is a breif introduction to several learning technologies with which we approach these questions.

Educational Technologies & Supporting Theories

Consumer Technologies (Cognitive Load Theory)
These technologies are used as tools in the classroom to create, share, & gather information.
Game Based Learning Environments (Scaffolding & Metacognition)
Exploiting the engagement, motivation, and challenge that comes instinctively to games, GBLE's promote active learning by students.
Intelligent Tutoring Systems (4C/ID Model)
ITS software aims to provide immediate and customized instruction or feedback to learners. These systems often try to mimic the feedback principles used by human tutors.
Informal Learning Technolgies (Social Cognitive Theory)
Informal learning technologies like those used at museums inspire visitors with wonder to promote intrinsic values of learning and social collaborative experiences.
