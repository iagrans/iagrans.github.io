An Overview of Intelligent Tutoring Systems

History has shown that students receiving expert, in-person tutoring, is highly effective to the learning process. Intelligent Tutoring Systems try to emulate best practices derived from the human model of tutoring. For example, the intelligent tutor can typically evaluate each step of the students learning process, and provide hints and feedback for the student to work the problem out themselves. Intelligent systems are distinguised from other computer-aided instruction in that they can interpret and respond to student interactions in real time by building a model of the student learner. Intelligent tutoring systems try to figure out not only if an answer is incorrect or not, but also how the student got to that understanding [1].

Intelligent tutors rely heavily on cognitive theories and practices in order to design effective systems. Some of the theories taken from cognitive science and educational psychology include minimizing the working memory load by providing a "scratch pad" for the learner to work on. Additionally, the tutors typically utilize problem solving behaviors which are goal driven. Furthermore, these goals can be linked together to develop approximations of target skills in the zone of proximal development [2]. Theories such as practice oppurtunities and scaffolding are all apart of the Four-Component Instructional Design (4C/ID) model [3]. The 4C/ID model supports that complex skills are learned by preforming them, so systems follwing the model focus on practicing problems more than mere presenatation of knowledge. The four components break down into the learning task, supportive information, just-in-time information, and part-task practice.

Applications in Practice

We will discuss how the 4C/ID model gets incorperated into tutoring systems by taking a closer look at the AutoTutor. AutoTutor is an intelligent tutoring system that helps students learn by holding a conversation in natural language [4]. An animated agent as seen to the left, acts as a dialogue partner for our user. The agent will try to elicit answers from the learner that exhibit deep reasoning (why and how), rather than shallow knowledge. AutoTutor has branched out to many different domains since its original inception as a computer literacy and conceptual physics tutor.

We can see the first component of the 4C/ID model, learning task, highlighted in AutoTutor through the break down of question components. AutoTutor presents the user with a series of problems that start off relatively simple, but then progress to more complex ones by adding in additional skills. For example, a problem set might escalate from simply finding the mass of an object to calculating its momentum as it strikes another hockey stick after being struck towards the goal. The system provides scaffolding to the students by providing hints and examples. The level of guidance fades as the system detects that the learner is coming closer to mastering a skill. Once one particular skill is mastered the student enters into a new problem set, where they might need more guidance in starting out.

To help both nonrecurrent skills (those that require much effort) and recurrent skills (those that require little to no effort) the AutoTutor implements components two and three of the 4C/ID model, supportive information and just-in-time information. Supportive information helps to bridge prior knowledge and new learning tasks in attempts to build schemata for nonrecurrent skills. By providing meaningful problems that relate to real world examples, the learners can take a concept skill and apply it elsewhere. Similarly, just-in-time information promotes recurrent automation by providing step-by-step guidance on fundamental tasks, and then fading quickly to free up cognitive resources for nonrecurrent problem-solving tasks.

We highlight the last component of van Meriënboer's 4C/ID model, part-task practice, in AutoTutor via the problem sets mentioned breifly above. Part-task-practice is a way of automatizing procedural knowledge more rapidly, while circumventing some of the cognitive load problems resulting when learners try to develop skills while simultaneously trying to solve a problem. In layman's terms this simply means, students keep practicing questions of similar form until the system decides that the student has mastered the skill. For example, the system might bring up several questions in a row that ask about the velocity of an object, only the inclince and distance is changing. The same problem solving techniques are used in each problem, and the student gets more practice developing automacticity of that skill.

Controversies Surrounding ITS

As intelligent tutpring systems is still a new field of practice, there remains controversy as to how effective they currently are. Dr. Elsom-Cook is one of the voice giving criticism on the use of student models in ITS systems.

These approaches both imply a very simplistic model of the learning process (not far removed from rote learning), which takes no account of the rich range of learning styles and capabilities for which there is psychological evidence.

elsom-cook

Futher criticisms lay with the interface itself, noting that words are not always what they appear at face value. While this is true, additional research has begun looking into tracking emtions while using intelligent tutoring systems and using that knowledge to better predict student behavior, future off-task behavior, and how to circumvent frustration [cite D'Mello].

Linguistic Research today is much more advanced, but "effective communication requires looking beyond the words that are spoken and determing what the tutor and student should be communicating about."

Woolf. Theoretical Problems of ITS, 1987

Outside of the system itself, there lies issue with students "gaming" the system. Complex intelligent tutors take years to build and polish. In some simpler tutoring systems, students might game the system by requesting hints and then ignoring them until the system offers the correct answer. Some educators believe that the provision of a correct answer—even after several hints—can rob students of their motivation to investigate, explore, and discover.
